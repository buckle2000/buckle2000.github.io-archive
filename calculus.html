Roadmap of AP Calculus

Continuity and Limits

Continuity and Limits are the basis of Calculus. Differentiation and integration depend on the continuity of their target functions. With continuity and limits, we can deal with infinite and infinitesinal, for example, determining the value approaching some point on a function even the point does not exist.

Derivatives

Derivatives are commonly know as "rate of change". They are derived from the concept of limits. In order to be differentiable, a function must be continuous.

Being the most pragmatic idea in Calculus, it is essential to both undergraduates and the work force.

Applications

Integrals

Integrals are the inverse form of derivatives. A integral assigns numbers to functions in a way that can describe displacement, area, volume, and other concepts that arise by combining infinitesimal data. In order to be integrable, a function must be continuous.

There is both definite integral (with interval) and indefinite integral (antiderivative). Variants of approximation for arithmetic evaluation also exist.

Applications

Transcendental Functions

A transcendental function is an analytic (antonym of algebraic) function that does not satisfy a polynomial equation, including trigonometric, exponential and logarithmic functions. It is not a subset of Calculus, yet it is used excessively throughout the Calculus system.

Transcendental Functions extend the frontier of derivatives and integrals to a more sophisticated and general-purpose level.

Infinite Series

Infinite series are polynomials with infinite terms. Rather than being continuous, series are rather discrete. This innovation of Calculus solves the problem of calculating transcendental functions with computers, which can only use 4 basic arithmetic operations.

Differential Equations

A differential equation is a mathematical equation that relates some function with its derivatives. They are commonly employed in many fields including flow dynamics, thermology and weather forecast.

Only the simplest differential equations are solvable by explicit formulas. Others solvable may be solved with aid of infinite series.

Applications

2D Vectors, Polar Functions

Vectors in plane can also be the subject of differentiation and integration. They can – and must – be used for real-life modeling, for instance, motions in 3D space.

Polar coordinate system is an alternative to Cartesian coordinate system. Quite often the universe has objects moving in circles and their dynamics can be simple and comprehensible using polar coordinates in favor of Cartesian ones.
